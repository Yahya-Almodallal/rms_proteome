Public proteomics workflows and datasets to evaluate MYMK and MYMX in rhabdomyosarcoma versus normal skeletal muscle
Executive summary
Public proteomics resources can support a reproducible comparison of MYMK (myomaker) and MYMX (myomixer) abundance between rhabdomyosarcoma (RMS) models and normal skeletal muscle, but the strategy must be tailored to the biology of these targets. MYMX is an 84–aa transmembrane microprotein (very small, hydrophobic), which makes it systematically under-detected by standard shotgun proteomics and especially hard to quantify across cohorts. 
 MYMK is a membrane fusogen (myoblast-fusion factor), likewise challenging for conventional bulk proteomics because hydrophobic, low-abundance, and often represented by few proteotypic tryptic peptides. 

For a practical, high-confidence workflow, the best starting point is to prioritize datasets that include RMS models and relevant normal muscle controls in the same experiment (minimizes batch effects) and/or membrane/surface-enriched workflows (higher odds of observing MYMK/MYMX peptides). The single most directly usable public dataset matching that profile is PXD039480, a surfaceome/membrane-enrichment proteomics study of multiple fusion-positive and fusion-negative RMS cell lines and PDXs with primary human myoblasts as controls, deposited to ProteomeXchange. 

To extend beyond a single study and benchmark RMS cell lines broadly, the most useful large-scale resource is CCLE deep mass-spec proteomics (TMT) in MassIVE MSV000085836 (375 cell lines), plus DepMap transcriptomics for RNA–protein concordance. 
 For normal skeletal muscle “ground truth,” PXD011967 (vastus lateralis biopsies from healthy participants; proteomics cohort includes 58 donors) and PXD034908 (SWATH time course in 18 healthy young men) provide widely-used muscle references, but cross-study comparisons require careful normalization and batch correction and are rarely reliable for single low-abundance membrane proteins.

Recommended repositories and programmatic discovery strategies
Even when you know the accession IDs above, reproducible work benefits from using a consistent “discovery → retrieval → analysis” approach across multiple repositories and mirrors.

Start discovery from ProteomeXchange (dataset announcements and centralized metadata), and use an index like OmicsDI to cross-query keywords (e.g., “rhabdomyosarcoma”, “Rh30”, “RD”, “surfaceome”, “phosphoproteome”). Public datasets are centrally browsable via ProteomeCentral and distributed across partner repositories and FTP locations. 

Recommended public ecosystems for this task (and why they matter):

ProteomeXchange Consortium: best for stable accession IDs (PXD) and standardized dataset announcements across repositories. 
PRIDE Archive: common host for human proteomics data and long-term FTP archival; many PXD projects resolve to PRIDE FTP downloads. 
MassIVE: critical for large-scale cell-line resources and some RMS projects; exposes MSV identifiers and (often) raw data. 
jPOSTrepo: hosts raw data for some cancer proteomics projects, distributed via FTP and mirrored in ProteomeCentral metadata (e.g., PXD035131). 
OmicsDI: cross-indexing of proteomics datasets plus a direct file listing for many PXD projects (useful when PRIDE pages are JS-heavy); shows file types and sometimes abstract sample numbers. 
Cancer Cell Line Encyclopedia and DepMap Portal: essential for mapping RMS cell lines (RD, RH30, etc.) to consistent identifiers, and for linking proteomics to transcriptomics and dependencies. 
UniProt: authoritative protein identifiers (accessions) and topology annotations needed for peptide-level validation (critical for MYMK/MYMX evidence review). 
Bioconductor: reproducible analysis ecosystem for differential abundance modeling and downstream QC/visualization via R packages (e.g., limma, MSnbase, MSstats).

Reproducible processing pipeline for MYMK/MYMX differential abundance
This section is structured as a workflow you can run end-to-end, with checkpoints specifically designed for “difficult” targets like MYMK/MYMX (few peptides, membrane localization, missingness).

Core pipeline decision: raw reanalysis vs processed tables
For MYMK/MYMX, you generally want raw reanalysis (or at least peptide-level processed outputs), because “protein-level only” tables may (a) drop low-confidence protein groups, (b) collapse ambiguous peptides, and (c) make it impossible to verify proteotypic evidence.

A practical compromise is:

Tier 1: Start from processed protein + peptide tables, if available (fast).
Tier 2: If MYMK/MYMX are absent or single-peptide, reanalyze raw files with a search tuned for small/membrane proteins, and extract peptide-level evidence.
Suggested search/quantification engines and why
Two widely used, reproducible open workflows:

MaxQuant for DDA-based LFQ or TMT, especially when you want “single-tool” standard outputs and broad community comparability. 
MSFragger (often via FragPipe) when you want fast searches and flexible search strategies (including open-search modes) and want to keep peptide-level control. 
For targeted validation (PRM/SRM), Skyline is a standard choice for method creation and quantitative analysis.

Handling shared peptides and “protein inference” for MYMK/MYMX
Because MYMK/MYMX are expected to yield few peptides, treat them as special-case targets:

Prefer unique (proteotypic) peptides mapping only to MYMK or MYMX.
If peptides are shared across protein groups, do not rely on a single protein-group intensity for biological conclusions; instead, report peptide-level evidence and ambiguity explicitly.
This is a practical constraint rather than a theoretical preference: it is the only way to avoid over-interpreting a protein-group call in a low-peptide regime.

Normalization, missingness, and imputation
Normalize intensities depending on design:
Within a single LFQ study: log2-transform protein intensities and apply global normalization (median/VSN-style) before modeling.
For multi-plex TMT: ensure the dataset has been normalized across plexes (many published workflows include a common reference channel); use the authors’ normalization if provided, and document it.
Downstream differential analysis in proteomics is strongly affected by missingness patterns; robust workflows explicitly address missing data rather than ignoring it. 

Imputation strategy (practical guidance):
For “missing at random” patterns (technical stochasticity): mild imputation can be acceptable.
For “missing not at random” patterns (low abundance below detection): left-censored imputation models are more defensible, but you must report sensitivity to imputation choices.
DEP-family workflows and benchmarking studies emphasize that imputation choices can materially change calls, especially for low-abundance proteins. 

Batch correction for cross-study comparisons
If you are comparing RMS datasets to separate normal muscle datasets (e.g., PXD039480 vs PXD011967), you will face dominant batch effects. If you still proceed:

Restrict to shared quantified peptides/proteins after harmonized processing if possible.
Run batch correction only after careful normalization and only when batch is known and not confounded with biology.
ComBat (empirical Bayes) is a standard method for adjusting known batch effects, implemented in the sva package. 

Statistical testing for differential abundance
Practical, reproducible options:

limma (moderated linear models) is robust for small n and flexible designs, and is commonly used in omics differential analyses. 
MSstats provides proteomics-focused modeling (linear mixed models) for label-free and labeled workflows and supports DDA/targeted/DIA inputs. 
For the “MYMK/MYMX question,” you will often be in a regime where per-protein statistics are underpowered (because the proteins may be missing or single-peptide). In that case, prioritize:

effect size estimates (log2 fold change),
presence/absence patterns,
peptide-level evidence strength,
and targeted validation plans (next section).
Strategies to validate low-abundance membrane proteins like MYMK and MYMX
MYMK/MYMX are muscle fusogens; MYMX is explicitly annotated as a short transmembrane protein (84 aa). 
 As a class, small proteins (<100 aa) are widely recognized as under-detected in conventional proteomics pipelines, which is directly relevant to MYMX.

Evidence hierarchy you should report for MYMK/MYMX
When you report “detected” or “not detected,” make it reproducible by explicitly logging:

Peptide evidence: number of unique peptides, peptide sequences, q-values/FDR, and whether peptides are modified.
Quantitation mode: MS1 intensity-based, spectral counting, or reporter-ion (TMT).
Spectrum-level support (when possible): use ProteomeXchange/USI workflows to point to representative spectra that support the peptide calls (especially if only 1 peptide). 
Why surfaceome/membrane enrichment datasets matter
Compared with whole-lysate shotgun proteomics, membrane/surface enrichment procedures can increase the probability of capturing transmembrane proteins. In practice, this is why PXD039480 is uniquely valuable for MYMK/MYMX: it enriched membrane/surface proteins, profiled multiple RMS lines/PDXs, and included primary myoblast controls in matched processing. 

Targeted reanalysis and targeted acquisition recommendations
If MYMK/MYMX are absent or single-peptide in discovery workflows, a pragmatic validation pathway is:

Targeted re-search of raw files (same data, different search assumptions) using a flexible engine such as MSFragger (e.g., alternate cleavage rules, open searches for unexpected modifications), then re-extract peptide evidence. 
Targeted acquisition (PRM/SRM) in new experiments:
Build a targeted method around candidate proteotypic peptides and transitions.
Quantify with Skyline (supports targeted method creation and quantitative analysis for SRM workflows; often used as the workbench for targeted assays). 
Use heavy-labeled peptide standards for absolute/relative quantification where feasible (especially if comparing across labs or batches).
Even if you do not run new MS now, designing a PRM/SRM panel is valuable because it forces you to answer the key feasibility question: are there proteotypic peptides for MYMK/MYMX in your protease/fragmentation context?

Integrating CCLE/DepMap proteomics and transcriptomics with normal muscle references
What CCLE/DepMap adds (and what it does not)
CCLE mass-spec proteomics provides a broad baseline of protein abundance across hundreds of cancer cell lines (MSV000085836; 375 lines, TMT 10-plex and SPS-MS3). 
DepMap provides harmonized cell line IDs and multi-omics layers (including transcriptomics), enabling protein–RNA concordance analysis and consistent filtering for RMS lineage. 
Sanger DepMap proteomics provides additional mass-spec proteomics at scale with raw files deposited as PXD030304, with processed data available per their documentation. 
What they don’t provide: true normal skeletal muscle proteomes processed alongside cancer cell lines. So CCLE/DepMap is best for RMS cell line benchmarking and RNA–protein concordance, not “RMS vs normal muscle tissue” without additional reference datasets and batch modeling.

A practical integration workflow
Identify RMS lines of interest (e.g., RD, RH30, Rh4, etc.) from PXD039480 (for matched RMS-vs-myoblast) and map them to CCLE/DepMap IDs. PXD039480 explicitly lists RD and Rh30 among the profiled RMS lines. 
Pull proteomics for those lines from:
MSV000085836 (CCLE proteomics) 
PXD030304 processed outputs (Sanger DepMap proteomics) 
Pull RNA expression (TPM/log2(TPM+1)) for MYMK/MYMX from DepMap releases and compute RNA–protein concordance:
Expect missing protein values even when RNA is present for MYMX-type microproteins (biological and technical limitations for small proteins). 
For normal muscle reference:
Use PXD011967 and/or PXD034908 to ask a different but critical question: are MYMK/MYMX peptides detectable in normal muscle proteomics at all, and under which acquisition type (DDA vs SWATH)? 
“RMS vs normal muscle” interpretation rules of thumb
If MYMX is not detected in muscle proteomics datasets where tens of hundreds to thousands of proteins are quantified, treat proteomics “non-detection” as weak evidence of absence for a microprotein, not confirmation of no expression. 
Stronger evidence comes from:
repeated detection of the same peptide(s) in multiple runs/replicates,
consistent quant signal across technical replicates,
and/or targeted validation.
Quality criteria, pitfalls, and a prioritized action plan with runnable tooling
Confidence criteria you should enforce for MYMK/MYMX calls
For each dataset and each target, document:

Whether the target is supported by ≥1 unique peptide at 1% FDR (or the dataset’s stated threshold).
Whether the identification is stable across replicates and conditions (especially in PXD039480 where triplicates exist). 
Whether the peptide(s) map uniquely in UniProt (to avoid protein inference artifacts). 
Whether signal is intensity-based or spectral-count based (spectral count quantification is more fragile for low counts, which is common for membrane proteins). 
Key pitfalls for this specific question
Missingness dominates: A “not quantified” protein is often a detection-limit issue, not a biological zero—especially for microproteins and membrane proteins. 
Cross-study batch effects: Comparing RMS cell lines (in vitro) to muscle biopsies (in vivo) across different labs and instruments is high risk for single-protein inference; if you do it, you must report the dependency on normalization/batch correction choices. 
Protein inference ambiguity: Small proteins can be “lost” if their peptides are shared, modified, low scoring, or filtered out. Treat peptide-level evidence as primary for MYMK/MYMX.
Prioritized action plan
This plan is designed to get you a defensible answer quickly, before you invest in cross-cohort harmonization.

First pass: single-study RMS vs myoblast (highest practicality)
Download and analyze PXD039480 first (RMS lines/PDXs + primary myoblast controls; membrane-enriched; triplicates). 
Extract MYMK/MYMX at both protein and peptide level; if absent, document absence and proceed to targeted re-search.
Second pass: broad RMS cell line benchmarking
Query CCLE proteomics (MSV000085836) for RMS lines and extract MYMK/MYMX; integrate with DepMap transcriptomics to see whether MYMK/MYMX RNA is present when protein is missing. 
Optionally expand to Sanger DepMap proteomics (PXD030304) for additional coverage and cross-platform confirmation. 
Third pass: normal muscle peptide detectability (supports interpretation)
Analyze PXD011967 (normal muscle; 58 donors) and PXD034908 (SWATH; 18 participants) specifically for MYMK/MYMX peptide detectability.
